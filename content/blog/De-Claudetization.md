Is AI actually making me more efficient? This question keeps coming up in my mind more and more often as time passes, and as the tokens I consume on Claude pile up.

Personally, and to keep things simple, the only metrics I'll consider, and I think they're representative enough to measure efficiency, are the ability to reach a goal and the time and energy spent doing it. For simplicity, in this article when I say "AI" I simply mean LLMs: ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini…

A few months/years ago the right move seemed to be using AI for (almost) everything: writing code, articles, emails, summarizing papers, recipes. It felt like the breakthrough, and I was hooked and fascinated by this revolutionary technology, convinced it was the revolution of human efficiency and that it would push the pace of development to all-time highs. And so it did! Right? Collectively yes, development and research have definitely grown in efficiency, my personal daily life probably not, or at least not at the expected pace.

In my opinion, from personal experience and observing the people around me, the cost has been steep, and a big part of it carries {{< mark >}}compound interest{{< /mark >}} that some users might pay dearly for in the years to come, momentarily fooled by their own productivity. That cost will show up as loss of logical reasoning, critical thinking, high self-distrust, little independence of action, personality and authenticity.

The common criticism we hear against AI users is the loss of skills and critical reasoning, maybe too obvious and simplistic as a critique. I'm sure it has to do with compulsive usage, but I'd rather focus on issues I've noticed in my own use of these LLMs, more original ones in my opinion.

---

## Distrust Toward Your Own Creations

One of the first problems I noticed in myself is the {{< mark >}}overuse of AI{{< /mark >}}: I no longer trust my own words, my thoughts, my way of articulating concepts. Even for simple emails I have to check with Claude, even just for a tone tweak or grammar corrections. It's a distrust toward ourselves and the content we produce.

Recently I made some strategic connections on LinkedIn to develop projects in the near future. Even though I had studied the people to reach out to and the messages to send, I had to ask Claude to validate my work, and maybe redo it from scratch, because I was afraid I had missed some information or detail. What did that get me? Distrust toward myself and my content, and a waste of time waiting for Claude's responses, writing endless prompts and going back and forth continuously. Remember the initial metrics: goal reached and time spent. The outcome stayed basically the same, but the time spent clearly increased, if not doubled.

We can split self-distrust into two modes: {{< mark >}}pathological and calibrated{{< /mark >}}. The calibrated one is justifiable, healthy, it's a distrust that comes from critical thinking to improve our own creations, reasoned and conscious. The pathological one, easy to guess from the name, is serious and chronic. It extends to grandma's recipe written on a piece of paper passed down for two generations: you ask Claude to confirm the measurements, times and ingredients. Confirmation on the tone of emails, on math formulas. It's a distrust that comes from the super-perfectionism of this technology, which ends up working against us. At the end of the day we are human beings, unique because we make mistakes and we are not perfect, we shouldn't run away from our errors in search of an artificial perfection.

---

## Overuse

To write an email, lately my process has been: explain the context, goal and recipient to Claude, get a first draft, figure out if it's what I wanted, and then another 2-3 correction prompts, not about the form of the email, which was probably already perfect, but about my idea and my goal. Unfortunately (luckily) Claude is not connected to my mind and doesn't pick up on all the human nuances or the ironies I'd want to include.

In the last few days I tried writing emails from scratch again. This allowed me to reach my goals much more quickly and easily, working actively on what I was doing and changing direction on the fly. What did I realize? That it makes no sense to use AI always and for everything, luckily, in many everyday human tasks we human beings are still stronger and more efficient. It should be used with critical thinking and we shouldn't rely on it too much.

Where does this habit of relying on AI for everything come from? {{< mark >}}Human beings are lazy{{< /mark >}}, they want to reach the goal with the least effort possible. But was I actually pulling it off? Is it worth losing 20 minutes correcting prompts for an email because we don't have clear ideas and we're not actively thinking it through, rather than spending 10 minutes of active work, where while writing the email we reason through our request with all the context of our life and our interactions with the recipient? Probably not. I promised myself I'd evaluate carefully and consciously when and whether to use AI, not for ethics, not for morality, but to figure out if it's actually making that task more efficient.

---

## Where Did the Wonder Go?

Reading content online has become flat --------------- completely flat. Everything in the same format, no grammar mistakes, no irony, no personality, everything perfect. The problem isn't just online, but also in essays, university assignments, podcasts, YouTube videos, everything follows a script created or at least reviewed and verified by AI (because, in my opinion, we're losing trust in ourselves and we think AI is the solution to optimize every process).

How do LLMs work? They're good, better than us, at predicting the next token. Full stop. They don't make mistakes, they don't produce outlier outputs, {{< mark >}}their output is always the median{{< /mark >}}, the most probable, the one that doesn't surprise, the one that doesn't stand out. It's easy to see that we're all becoming copies of each other, or at least our online content is, and it no longer reflects who we really are.

It's become hard to feel wonder: everything is monotonous. You read a LinkedIn post and feel like you're having a flashback, that feeling of having already lived that moment, but then you go back to the previous post and it was genuinely identical.

---

*Small tangent: my goal in the coming months is to focus more on my blog on the Personal Shared Web Space and grow my content on LinkedIn. Lately I've found a lot of value in it and I want to make the most of it. I'll commit to creating content avoiding AI as much as possible, surely with grammar mistakes, less fluency, but hoping it can transmit something more to the reader: something personal and authentic.*

---

So are LLM trash and all hype? No, I'm convinced it's the breakthrough and it remains the revolution of human efficiency. It's up to us, as always, to set our own limits and put our heads into what we're doing. It's still an incredibly powerful technology that lets us build incredibly complex software projects in an afternoon, solve mechanical problems, summarize, translate, study, understand physics concepts in a simple way, etc...

**Key takeaway?** What I'll try to do in the coming days is think actively before asking Claude a question: is it worth it? Is this a task that actually requires AI, or am I more efficient and can I reach the same goal in less time without wasting energy and without losing critical thinking? I'll try to use AI only in the tasks where it genuinely makes my processes more efficient, without undermining my work and my abilities.

Ah, in case it wasn't clear - I'm a Claude fanboy ;)
