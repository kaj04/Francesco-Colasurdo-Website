There's always a downside, and this one is worth pointing out.

It's a small critique I had already formed before the hackathon, and one the weekend confirmed and deepened.

{{< mark >}}AI is becoming like parsley{{< /mark >}}. In southern Italy we have a saying: *"staij ind a tutt i minestr comm u pretusin"*, literally "you're in every soup like parsley." AI is incredibly powerful, a tool that is and will continue to allow us to dramatically increase efficiency across our society in many ways. But in my opinion, we shouldn't force it or feel obligated to use it everywhere, because sometimes it becomes counterproductive, overcomplicating problems and spending more (computationally, or in terms of the user's learning curve) than necessary.

Which brings me to what I observed at the hackathon: {{< mark >}}none of the solutions (including ours) really stood out for innovation or genuine use of multimodal models{{< /mark >}}. I'm not criticising anyone here, it's just a personal take, and I'm genuinely curious about opposing views. But all the solutions that were awarded, and all those I could see on Devpost, don't actually require real multimodal reasoning. Some more than others tried to squeeze a "multimodal" angle into a problem that had little to do with those technologies. Those who were more creative genuinely found the right problems within Bunq's product, ones that actually called for multimodal AI rather than simpler alternatives. The majority, in my view, forced the technology into their solution.

The short version: {{< mark >}}the best solution is often the simplest one{{< /mark >}}. If a problem can be solved with a simple script or a basic technology, there's no need to overcomplicate it just because saying you're "using AI" sounds impressive.

Keep it simple!
